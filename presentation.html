Legal Background

Criminal and Civil Cases

In the U.S. Justice System, there are two forms of legal proceedings; criminal law and civil law.

  • Criminal Law
  • Civil Law
  • The defendant is presumed innocent until proven guilty of a criminal act.
  • The burden of proof is on the prosecution to prove that the defendant is guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.

  • The defendant is accused of causing harm and/or damages through their actions.
  • The plaintiff must show that the defendant is likely liable for damages based on a “preponderance of the evidence”.
  • If the defendant is found liable, the court will determine a legal remedy to compensate for the harm to the plaintiff(s).

Proving Discrimination

  • Legally, discrimination occurs when a person is unfairly disadvantaged due to a protected ground, such as:
    • Age
    • Sex
    • Gender identity/presentation
    • Race
  • Note: Discrimination does not have to be intentional. The legal definition considers impact, not intent.
  • Discrimination lawsuits are primarily handled in civil courts, where the burden of proof is a balance of probabilities.

Statistical Interpretation

  • We can consider these discrimination lawsuits under a hypothesis testing framework.
  • The null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) is that there is no discrimination in the provided scenario.
    • Rejecting \(H_0\) is equivalent to finding the defendant liable.
    • Failing to reject \(H_0\) means that there is not sufficient enough evidence to convince the court of discrimination.

Testing for Discrimination

Discrimination in Employee Layoffs

  • Council 31 v. Ward (1991) is a legal case from Illinois concerning a mass layoff of over 200 government employees.
  • 40.1% of the fired employees were Black, despite the fact that Black employees comprised only 34.3% of the workforce.
  • The plaintiffs argued that these layoffs discriminated against Black employees due to their race.

Applying Relative Risk

  • The standard in many discrimination cases is to examine the relative risk between the groups.
  • Relative risk is a ratio which compares the likelihood of an event or outcome across multiple groups.

The Odds Ratio

  • The odds ratio is a measurement which can be used to quantify the association between a pair of events.
  • In this situation, the odds ratio may be a better metric for assessing whether these layoffs were discriminatory towards Black employees.
  • In this legal case, the odds ratio is \(\frac{130/1382}{87/2813} = 3.04\).
    • This signifies a strong association between an employee being Black and an employee being fired.

Fisher’s Exact Test

  • Fisher’s Exact Test is a statistical test which is used for analyzing contingency tables.
  • The test assumes that the margins of the table are fixed.
  • If employees are fired “fairly” (without discrimination based on race), the odds ratio should be close to 1.
    • We can write this as a null hypothesis; \(H_0: \text{OR} = 1\), with \(H_A: \text{OR} \ne 1\).

Calculating the Remedy

Remedying a Salary Gap

  • EEOC 1 vs. Shelby County (1988) is a case from Tennessee concerning unequal pay for male and female employees of the County.
  • In this lawsuit, the remedy was determined using statistical modelling.

Investigating the Pay Inequality

  • In this lawsuit, the disparate impact of discrimination is the difference in monthly salary between male and female employees.

  • Linear regression can be used to determine the significance of this difference.

    • The null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) is that sex does not impact salary.






The p-value corresponding to the Sex variable is 1.86e-05.



The estimated effect of Sex on salary is $199.11 per month.

Peters-Belson Regression

  • Based on these outputs, it seems that the average female employee makes $199.11 less per month than a male employee with the same seniority level.
  • The legal remedy could be to pay each female employee $199.11 per month to make up the difference.
    • However, seniority is also linearly correlated with the monthly salary.
  • Peters-Belsen regression estimates the salary which each female employee would have earned, if they were paid like male employees.
    • The legal remedy for each female employee is the difference between the earned salary and their deserved salary.

Key Messages

Key Messages

  • Statistical methods and models are highly applicable in legal cases, particularly in civil lawsuits.
  • We can view these civil lawsuits using a hypothesis test.
  • Fisher’s Exact Test can determine if there is a disparate impact between groups.
    • The null hypothesis (\(H_0\)) is that there is no disparate impact; the odds ratio is 1.
    • Fisher’s Exact Test is best for 2x2 contingency tables, but it still works for larger tables.
  • We can use Peters-Belson regression to determine the legal remedy for a disparate impact in salary or payment.
    • PB regression is fitted based only on the values from the non-impacted group.
    • The estimated outcomes from PB regression represent the expected salary of someone who is not discriminated against.
    • The legal remedy ensures that all employees are paid as if they are members of the “majority group”.
